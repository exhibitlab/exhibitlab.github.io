MASONRY

The broad category of “masonry” includes brick, hollow concrete blocks, adobe, clay, and stone. The most common masonry buildings in the United States are made of brick, used for its durability, fire resistance, and aesthetic. In order to increase strength, masonry can be reinforced, often by horizontal and vertical steel bars sandwiched between two layers of masonry. Masonry is very strong in compression, but has no tensile strength, and when forces are applied that are stronger than the masonry, they break very quickly and suddenly, called brittle failure. Unreinforced masonry buildings perform very poorly in earthquakes because the material itself is very heavy, leading to high inertial forces, and the walls themselves are not good in resisting horizontal forces, which is how the majority of buildings sustain their damage from earthquakes. For these reasons, the construction of unreinforced masonry buildings in seismic heavy zones is illegal.




WOOD

Wood is a lightweight, flexible material often used for low-rise residential buildings, such as single family homes and apartments, as well as some office buildings. When tested, wood was able to carry a larger load when over a shorter period of time than a longer period, which makes it a very viable material when considering loading from earthquakes. However, wood is mostly used for low rise buildings because it has not been researched as a material to the extent that concrete and steel have been.











CONCRETE

Concrete is a mixture of fine and coarse aggregates, water, and cement, and the mix is poured into forms or molds so that when the concrete hardens, it takes the proper shape of the form. Other additives can be included in the mix to improve the properties of concrete, such as its strength. The materials that make up concrete themselves are not expensive, but the formwork that the concrete is poured in can be quite expensive depending on the complexity. Concrete is very strong in compression, but weak in tension. To increase the tensile strength, reinforcement is added to concrete in the form of steel bars. The concrete is poured around the steel bars so that when the concrete hardens, it fully surrounds and bonds to the reinforcing steel, allowing the two materials to behave as one when loads are applied.





STEEL

Steel has a high strength-to-weight ratio, making it very cost-effective for large projects, such as skyscrapers. It is a very strong and ductile material, which allows it to keep on carrying loads after it bends. Unlike concrete, steel is very good in both tension and compression. In terms of sustainability, around 90% of structural steel is from recycled steel. Steel is a non-combustible material, but at very high temperatures, steel will lose a significant amount of strength, so some form of fireproofing is required when constructing steel buildings.
