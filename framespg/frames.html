Conventional Earthquake Resisting Systems

When designing for the long term, structural engineers tend to lean towards conventional earthquake resisting systems due to their performance-based and cost effective characteristics

There are three types of conventional systems:

Steel Moment Resisting Frames (MRF)
Steel Concentrically Braced Frames (CBF)
Concrete Shear Walls (SW)

These systems are able to resist lateral forces with their strong stiffness and dissipate energy through their
inelastic behavior when deforming during an earthquake, thus permanently damaging the structural elements.

Every type has a specific fuse mechanism used to dissipate the energy produced by the earthquake in the building. However, all the fuses’ capacity for conventional earthquake resisting systems have a limit and get permanently damaged.



Steel Moment Frames


  • These frames are ideal to counteract intense ground motion, since their rigid joints and flexible members can resist rotation at connections caused by earthquakes.
  • MRFs dissipate energy by the bending behavior, or “flexural yielding” that can happen at the connection between beams and columns.

    • ×

    Fun Fact

    This connection was very common before the Northridge earthquake, which failed afterward from brittle fracture. Alternatives to reduce this possibility were considered such as welding with strong fracture toughness, inspection, or using prequalified connections by AISC.

     

    Braced Frames

  • Visually similar to moment frames, they have members in the center of the openings that help support some of the lateral forces and therefore decrease the column sizes. Another differentiation is that braced frames do not use any moment connections, making them a preferable option over moment frames.

  • CBFs dissipate energy by tensile yielding and compressive buckling of braces

    • Reinforced concrete shear walls

  • Shear walls act as a vertical diaphragm to resist lateral forces, especially important in high-rise buildings.

  • SWs dissipate energy by the cracking and crushing of concrete at the base of the wall and yielding of reinforcing steel placed inside the wall
