Types of Braced Frames

Lateral Force Resisting
Systems (LRFS)

LRFS are highly performance-base and cost effective, where the three main types are moment frames, braced frames, and shear walls. Continue to explore the pros and cons for moment frames and the different types of braced frames based on the following criteria: stiffness, functionality, strength, cost, and aesthetics.

Moment Frames

  • Modern moment frames are used mostly for window design, due to their limited usefullness
  • Fuses: Bending, “flexural yield,” can happen at end of beams
  • Advantages: Flexibility in architectural design, economical foundation systems, strong columns
  • Disadvantages: difficult electrical coordination, heavier sections, labor intensive connections and special detailing, increased costs, drift control problems, weak beam


    • Braced Frames

  • Braced Frames look like moment frames, however they have members in the center of the openings that help support some of the lateral forces and therefore making engineers decrease the column sizes. Another differentiation is that braced frames do not use moment connections, making them a preferable option over moment frames.

    Concentrically Braced Frames (CBF)

  • In CBF, the two members in the openings of the frame meet at the same point.
  • Fuses: tensile yield and compressive buckling of braces
  • Advantages: very stiff elastically, tension yielding of brace is desirable, no need for moment connection
  • Disadvantages: blocks passage of ducts, obstacle for nice views, brace elastic buckling might govern, large connection, required fireproofing of steel member







    • Buckling-Restrained Brace (BRB)

  • BRB is similar to the CBF, except with the addition of steel core within braces that help restraining the buckling issue that CBF faced.
  • Fuses: steel core within braces
  • Advantages: very stiff laterally, no elastic buckling, simple repairment from earthquakes
  • Disadvantages: blocks passage of ducts, obstacle for nice views, limitation based on manufacturing

    • Eccentrically Braced Frames (EBF)

  • EBF adds a link panel in between the two members in the opening.
  • Fuses: yield in link panel
  • Advantages: very stiff elastically, small connections
  • Disadvantages: stiffness and ductility are affected by the length of the link







    • Controlled Rocking Steel Braced Frame (CRSBF)

  • Fuse: replaceable fuse
  • Advantages: The frame is isolated from the foundation, only the PT strands are connected to the foundation, presence of fuse to absorb most of the energy, replaceable fuse, low to no permanent displacement “residual drift”
  • Disadvantages: blocks passage of ducts, obstacle for nice views, applicable to or less than 9 stories
