ALL HAIL

Economic Impact

The earthquake-associated economic losses are determined by the amount of damage the whole 6-story building is projected to sustain.

How we begin the analysis

The building is broken down into its damageable components, some of which include the CRSBF itself, walls, suspended ceilings, and piping.

Fragility Curve

Each of these components has a corresponding fragility curve, which estimates the amount of damage to the component depending on the earthquake intensity, which then can be used to generate descriptions of the physical damage to the component.

Anaysis

Component loss functions then use the descriptions to determine the economic cost of the overall building. The economic loss is then normalized by the cost of replacement, excluding demolition.

Environmental Impact

Life Cycle Assessment

A Life Cycle Assessment is used to determine the environmental impact of both the initial construction and whatever repairs and replacements are necessary following an earthquake.

A life-cycle assessment accounts for the various stages involved in this construction process, from the securing of raw materials, processing to create a product, transportation to the construction site, and the installation itself.

ATHENA

For this particular building, the ATHENA software was used to calculate the environmental impacts in terms of global warming potential in kg equivalent of CO2 produced and the energy consumed.

Specific Energy and CO2 Output

This economic assessment provides the quantity of each component, including partial walls, glass curtain walls, and HVAC ducting, that was installed and later will be repaired after an earthquake.

The environmental impact corresponding to each component can then be calculated based on an extensive material database that lists how much CO2 and energy is emitted and consumed respectively for a unit quantity.

Cost

The repair and replacement costs of these materials are calculated using RS Means, a construction cost of each damageable building component.
