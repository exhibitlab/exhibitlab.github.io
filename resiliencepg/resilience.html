Key Terms

Resilience and Sustainable Buildings

When thinking about the future of the next generation of buildings, a major challenge structural engineers are facing is ensuring both the resiliency and sustainability of buildings.

The two concepts are complementary, but sometimes design decisions, instead of increasing both, will increase one and decrease the other. In such a case, the focus on one or the other depends on the kind of project and the owner’s wishes.

Resilience

Resilience is concerned with the impact that one extreme event has on a structure and the structure’s capacity to withstand the event and recover from the aftermath. Resilient buildings are stiff enough to provide serviceability and strong enough to resist all the applied loads. For example, resilience can be met with more members, bigger connections, and heavier sections. However, all these design changes can drastically increase the costs of the design and the potential repair costs after damage as there could be more members to fix.

Sustainability

Sustainability is maintaining the building and its surroundings in good shape for a lifetime by incorporating practices that reduce environmental and economical impacts of the building. Its primary concern is to ensure that the current building is functional and meets its needs without compromising the future ability to meet needs. While resilience is mainly concerned with present strength and recovery, sustainability reduces impact for the future. Resilience can be applied to a broader community perspective as well, as the damage that a structure sustains can be measured by the impact it has on the surrounding community, and how quickly and efficiently that community can recover.
