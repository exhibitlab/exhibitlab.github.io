Earthquake Timeline

Scroll down to explore the major earthquakes that shook the US from the 20th century and onward.

San Francisco Earthquake
7.9 magnitude

  • Building located on “filled” soil performed worse than buildings on natural ground
  • Buildings with steel frames and unreinforced masonry infill walls performed the best, other buildings (wood frames and unreinforced stone and brick masonry) performed worse
  • Led to building codes requiring tall buildings to have complete vertical load-carrying space frames and buildings over 240 ft to have moment resisting space frames
  • More than 3,000 people died

    • April 18, 1906

    Long Island Earthquake
    6.3 magnitude

  • Many unreinforced masonry buildings were damaged
  • 75% of schools in the area were damaged or destroyed
  • Caused California legislators to prohibit the construction of more unreinforced masonry buildings and enabled requirements for high-quality design and construction of schools
  • Set a precedent for the prohibition of certain structural systems and acknowledgement that some buildings and their occupants were more important that others and should be designed to be safer
  • Resulted in the first mandatory seismic design code in the US
  • 115 to 120 people died
  • $40 millions of damaged

    • March 10, 1933

    El Centro Earthquake
    7.0 magnitude

  • Caused widespread damage to irrigation systems
  • First strong test of schools designed to be earthquake-resistant after the 1933 Long Beach earthquake, 15 of these had no apparent damage
  • USGS recorded very high-quality, strong ground motion data
  • Most of early work by earthquake engineering researchers was from these strong ground motions
  • 9 people died, and 20 injured
  • $6 millions of damage

    • May 18, 1940

    Kern Country Earthquake
    7.4 magnitude

  • Well-designed buildings had slight damage, but old and poorly-built buildings were damaged or destroyed
  • Strong ground motion data also heavily used for research
  • 14 people died and 100 injured
  • $70 millions of damage

    • July 21, 1952

    San Fernando Earthquake
    6.6 magnitude

  • Damaged many code-conforming buildings that engineers thought were earthquake-resistant
  • Olive View Medical Building: wings pulled away from the main building, several stair towers collapsed, first story was crushed
  • Recognized the importance of detailing in relation to performance in earthquakes
  • Occupancy categories and site classes were introduced into the building code
  • About 65 people died and 2,000 injured
  • $505-553 millions of damage

    • February 9, 1971

    Imperial Valley Earthquake
    6.4 magnitude

  • Relatively small amount of structural damage
  • Imperial County Services Building: all of the east end first-floor columns were crushed due to overturning moment, exterior east wall also started to lean due to this
  • Led building code to require the design of columns under discontinuities to include the overstrength factor of the building above
  • 91 people injured
  • $30 millions of damage

    • October 15, 1979

    Mexico City Earthquake
    8.1 magnitude

  • Over 1,000 masonry and reinforced concrete buildings were damaged or destroyed
  • 2 high-rise steel buildings collapsed due to local buckling near the plates of the steel box columns, which caused the seams of the columns to separate and the columns to buckle
  • Resulted in the building code requiring columns to be able to resist the maximum axial load that would be delivered to the columns
  • More than 5,000 people died and 30,000 injured
  • $4 billions of damage

    • September 19, 1985

    Loma Prieta Earthquake
    7.1 magnitude

  • 28 buildings recorded ground motion data, which helped with design and analysis procedures. It additionally allowed engineers to study the earthquake itself to better predict performance in future high-magnitude earthquakes
  • Modern buildings were relatively undamaged, but older buildings suffered a lot of damage
  • Data gathered from this earthquake and earlier earthquakes used to develop Site Class factors in the building code and numerical models to help simulate earthquakes for analysis
  • 63 killed and 3,757 injured
  • $6 billions of damage

    • August 8, 1989

    Northridge Earthquake
    6.7 magnitude

  • Several portions of major freeways collapsed
  • Discovered fractures in the connections of modern steel buildings
  • Changes in practice: introduction of a new grade of structural steel with controlled strength, requirements to accurately test connections, use weld filler of a certain toughness, and remove weld tabs after welding
  • Building code changed to require redundancy in structural design, as it was observed that buildings with more redundancies fared better
  • 57 killed and more than 8,700 injured
  • $13-50 billions of damage

    • January 17, 1994
